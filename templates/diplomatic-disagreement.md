# Diplomatic Disagreement Phrasing

Reference for `/revise` Step 6. Use when a referee comment is classified DISAGREE.
**Never use without user approval.** Flag the draft and wait.

---

## The Structure of a Good Diplomatic Pushback

1. **Acknowledge** — show you understood the concern
2. **Restate your position** — with evidence, not assertion
3. **Offer a partial concession** — if you can do something, do it
4. **Close professionally** — leave the door open

Never: "The referee is wrong." "We disagree." "This is not correct."
Always: Make the referee feel heard while defending the substance.

---

## Templates by Situation

### Situation 1: The referee requests a robustness check you've already done

> We thank the referee for this suggestion. We did examine [X] in an earlier draft and found
> [result]. We have added this as Table A[N] in the Online Appendix and note the result in
> Section [Y] (p. [Z]). [One sentence interpreting the result and why it supports/complicates
> your main finding.]

---

### Situation 2: The referee requests a specification you believe is misidentified

> We appreciate the referee's suggestion to [include X / use specification Y]. We are concerned,
> however, that this approach may not be well-identified because [specific reason: omitted
> variable, reverse causality, etc.]. [One sentence citing a paper that makes this point if
> available.] We have added a footnote in Section [X] (p. [Y]) explaining this concern and
> noting that [alternative approach] addresses it while preserving identification. We hope this
> addresses the spirit of the referee's comment.

---

### Situation 3: The referee cites a paper that contradicts your finding

> We thank the referee for drawing our attention to [Author et al., Year]. We have read this
> paper carefully. [One sentence: how does it differ? Different sample? Different time period?
> Different measure?] We believe our finding and theirs are reconcilable because [specific
> reason]. We have added a paragraph in Section [X] (p. [Y]) discussing this comparison
> directly.

---

### Situation 4: The referee asks for data you do not have access to

> We agree that [data/variable X] would strengthen the analysis. Unfortunately, [explain why
> you cannot access it: DUA restrictions, data not collected, not yet publicly available].
> As an alternative, we use [proxy/indirect measure] in Table A[N], which speaks to the same
> concern. We have added a discussion of this limitation in Section [X] (p. [Y]).

---

### Situation 5: The referee raises a theoretical concern that doesn't apply to your setting

> We appreciate the referee's theoretical concern about [X]. This would be a binding constraint
> if [condition], but our setting differs in that [specific institutional/empirical feature that
> rules it out]. We have added a paragraph in Section [X] (p. [Y]) explaining why [condition]
> does not hold here and providing supporting evidence from [source].

---

### Situation 6: The referee wants major restructuring you believe is unnecessary

> We have carefully considered the referee's suggestion to [restructure X]. We are sympathetic
> to the underlying motivation — [restate it generously]. After discussion among the authors,
> we believe the current structure better serves [specific goal: the identification narrative /
> the reader unfamiliar with the literature / the contribution to X]. To address the referee's
> concern more directly, we have [made a smaller change: added a roadmap paragraph /
> clarified the contribution statement / added a section header]. We hope this achieves
> the clarity the referee was looking for.

---

## Phrases to Avoid

| Avoid | Use Instead |
|-------|-------------|
| "The referee is mistaken" | "We may not have been clear about..." |
| "We disagree" | "We see this differently because..." |
| "This is not correct" | "Our reading of the evidence is..." |
| "The referee misunderstood" | "We have revised to clarify..." |
| "We already addressed this" | "As noted in Section X (p. Y)..." |
| "This is outside our scope" | "We have added a footnote noting..." |

---

## When to Truly Push Back

Reserve direct (but still diplomatic) pushback for:
- Requests that would require fundamentally different data
- Requests based on a factual error in the referee's reading
- Requests that contradict the stated scope of the journal

In these cases, be clear but not combative. Document the disagreement in the tracker
and flag it for the user — they should read and approve the response before it goes out.
