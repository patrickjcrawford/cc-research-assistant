# Cleanup Pass -- AI Writing Pattern Detection

After completing a draft, run a cleanup pass to strip residual AI writing patterns. This is a polish step -- the argument moves are the primary drafting strategy.

---

## Anti-Hedging (enforced)

Remove these phrases on sight:
- "interestingly"
- "it is worth noting"
- "arguably"
- "it is important to note"
- "it should be noted"
- "needless to say"

---

## 24 AI Patterns, 4 Categories

### Category 1: Content Patterns (6 patterns)

1. **Significance inflation** -- "pivotal moment", "crucial juncture", "paradigm shift" when describing routine findings
2. **Promotional language** -- "groundbreaking", "remarkable", "unprecedented" applied to modest contributions
3. **Superficial -ing analyses** -- "highlighting the importance of...", "underscoring the need for..." as sentence fillers
4. **Vague attributions** -- "experts argue", "scholars have noted", "research suggests" without specific citations
5. **False breadth** -- "across a wide range of contexts", "in numerous settings" when the paper covers one setting
6. **Unearned certainty** -- "clearly demonstrates", "definitively establishes" for correlational evidence

### Category 2: Language Patterns (6 patterns)

7. **AI vocabulary** -- additionally, delve, foster, garner, interplay, tapestry, underscore, landscape, nuanced, leverage, multifaceted, holistic
8. **Copula avoidance** -- "serves as" instead of "is", "functions as" instead of "is"
9. **Negative parallelisms** -- "not only X but also Y" used repeatedly
10. **Excessive hedging** -- stacking qualifiers: "it may potentially be the case that..."
11. **Nominalizations** -- "the implementation of" instead of "implementing", "the utilization of" instead of "using"
12. **Faux-academic connectors** -- "moreover", "furthermore", "in light of" used mechanically every paragraph

### Category 3: Style Patterns (6 patterns)

13. **Em dash overuse** -- more than 2 em dashes per page signals AI drafting
14. **Rule of three** -- three examples/adjectives/phrases grouped together, every time
15. **Uniform sentence length** -- all sentences 15--25 words, no variation
16. **Forced parallel structure** -- every list or comparison in identical grammatical form
17. **Opening with gerunds** -- "Building on...", "Drawing from...", "Leveraging..." to start paragraphs
18. **Closing with forward references** -- "The next section explores..." at the end of every section

### Category 4: Communication Patterns (6 patterns)

19. **Filler phrases** -- "It's important to note that...", "It bears mentioning that..."
20. **Meta-commentary** -- "This section discusses...", "We now turn to..." as paragraph openers
21. **Artificial transitions** -- "Having established X, we now examine Y" between every section
22. **Summary repetition** -- restating the paragraph's point in the last sentence
23. **Rhetorical questions** -- "What does this imply?" used as transitions
24. **Catalogue sentences** -- "Several factors contribute to this phenomenon, including A, B, C, D, and E" with no development

---

## Academic Adaptation Rules

The cleanup pass must NOT:
- Remove formal register (no forced casualness)
- Simplify technical estimator names
- Strip citations that serve a positioning function
- Over-correct domain-specific hedging that is genuinely appropriate (e.g., "may" when describing potential mechanisms is fine in economics)

**Target voice:** Reads like an economist who writes clearly, not like a machine that avoids tells.

---

## Cleanup Protocol

1. Run through all 24 patterns against the drafted text
2. Flag each instance found
3. Replace or delete -- do not rewrite surrounding sentences
4. Preserve the argument structure (paragraph moves) intact
5. Re-read the cleaned text to verify it still flows naturally
